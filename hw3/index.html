Overview

In this homework, we implemented Monte Carlo path tracing to simulate more realistic lighting than regular rasterization could allow. We started with camera ray generation and primitive intersections, then added BVH acceleration to make rendering complex scenes practical. From there, we introduced direct lighting through diffuse BSDFs, zero-bounce emission, hemisphere sampling, and light importance sampling, and extended that into global illumination by adding indirect lighting with ray bounces. Finally, adaptive sampling made the renderer more efficient by spending more samples on noisy pixels and fewer on pixels that had already converged. As a former avid PC gamer who never upgraded to an RTX card from my GTX 1070, this was a really exciting assignment for me - ray tracing has been a pretty-looking but unobtainable reality for me for years, so it is very interesting to be able to see how it works and implement it myself!

Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

The rendering pipeline begins in PathTracer::raytrace_pixel, where for each pixel, ns_aa camera samples are generated by drawing random offsets inside the pixel's unit square. Each sample point's coordinates are then normalized by dividing by the image width and height.

These normalized coordinates are then passed to Camera::generate_ray, where the image is mapped from [0x1] x [0x1] into the virtual sensor in camera space on the plane z = -1. The camera is then set at its origin and a ray is calculated of its direction.

Rays are now sent to est_radiance_global_illumination, which depends on primitive intersection to find the rays' nearest hit. Candidate primitives each check whether the ray intersects it within [min_t, max_t]. When a valid hit is found, the primitive updates max_t to its hit distance t. This ensures the final result is always the closest visible surface, and further objects are ignored.

Triangle intersection is implemented using the Moller-Trumbore algorithm. We first form the triangle edge vectors and compute a determinant to determine if the ray is parallel to the triangle - if near zero, they are parallel, and there is no hit. Otherwise, barycentrics b1 and b2 are computed and their validity as inside the triangle verified. Finally, t is computed and updated if necessary.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

The BVH is constructed recursively. With each recursive call, we first compute a bounding box that encloses every primitive in the current range, and a second bounding box over the centroids of those primitive bounding boxes. If the current range contains at most max_leaf_size primitives, we stop and create a leaf node. Otherwise, we choose the split axis as the axis with the largest centroid extent to separate the primitives along the direction where they are most spread. We then split at the midpoint of the centroid bounding box on that axis and partition the primitive range into left and right subsets by checking the location of each primitive centroid against the split point. This only fails if all centroids fall on one side, leaving one subset empty and inviting infinite recursion! To fix this, if partitioning produces an empty side, we split by the median primitive using std::nth_element to guarantee both at least one primitive for left and right.

BVH implementation significantly reduced rendering times for all scenes. Without BVH, each camera ray had to test against every primitive, making render time scale linearly with complexity. Now, we can skip groups of primitives whenever possible by first testing against bounding boxes. The larger the scene, the greater the benefit, as we can discard more of the geometry we do not need. For comparison, render times in seconds (pre-BVH : post-BVH) for maxplanck (220.7697 : 0.0903) and CBdragon (448.9189 : 0.0680) both dropped significantly.

Part 3: Direct Illumination

Direct illumination now generates a camera ray for each pixel sample and intersects it against the BVH. If the ray hits a surface, we first compute the zero-bounce radiance (surface emission), allowing objects like the Cornell box ceiling light to be visible. One-bounce radiance then estimates how much light arrives at the hit point directly from the scene lights and is reflected toward the camera by the surface Lambertian BSDF.

In uniform hemisphere sampling, we sample directions uniformly over the normal points along local +z above the surface. For each sampled direction, we then cast a shadow ray from the hit point; if it hits an emissive surface, we treat the emitted radiance as incoming light and find the Monte Carlo estimate. Because the hemisphere samples are uniform, the pdf is 1/2pi. After summing over all the samples, we divide by the number of samples.

In light sampling, we loop over each light in the scene and sample directions from the light directly, giving its direction, distance, radiance, and pdf. We then cast a shadow ray toward the light to determine if it is visible from the hit point - if it does not intersect any object, it is visible, and we add the Monte Carlo estimate.

While uniform hemisphere sampling is more simple, it is inefficient as most sampled directions do not hit a light source. This means many samples contribute nothing, which results in noisy images and slow convergence. Light sampling is much more effective as every sample is, as the name suggests, chosen from an actual light source. This results in much cleaner shadows and less noise, as is visible in the comparison renders of the bunny and dragon below.

As seen below, increasing the number of light rays results in a progressively less noisy image.

Part 4: Global Illumination

The indirect lighting function at_least_one_bounce_radiance estimates the global illumination at a specific intersection point by recursively tracing rays. We first establish our coordinate spaces and compute the direct lighting here using one_bounce_radiance, then add it to our total output radiance if accumulating bounces (otherwise, we only store it if the ray depth is 1 to isolate the exact nth bounce). Rays are initialized with a depth of max_ray_depth, which is decremented on each bounce. Recursion ends when the ray reaches its final bounce at a depth of 1. Russian roulette is implemented here with a continuation probability of 0.65 to ensure there is never infinite recursion, but we still guarantee at least one indirect bounce regardless with the force_bounce condition. If the ray survives, we check with the material's BSDF, and trace the next ray if the fetched pdf is greater than zero. This new ray can then be cast into the scene using the BVH, where if it hits an object, we recurse over the function again to determine the effects of that new intersection. Finally, the Monte Carlo estimator can be used to determine L_out for the ray, which we can then normalize and accumulate for a final result as necessary.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

In Monte Carlo path tracing, we eliminate noise by tracing multiple rays through each pixel and averaging their resulting radiance. However, not all pixels are equally complex - a pixel on a flat wall will need far fewer samples than one on a shadow edge, for example. A fixed high sampling rate wastes lots of time and power on easier pixels that already converged, while a fixed lower sampling rate will not be sufficient for more difficult pixels. Adaptive sampling solves this by evaluating the variance of the light arriving at each pixel to determine when the pixel converges and move on to the next pixel accordingly.

This is adapted into raytrace_pixel in this project. We need three new tracking variables, s1 (sum of samples' illuminances), s2 (sum of samples' squared illuminances), and actual_samples (counter of actual samples rather than the now maximum samples parameter). To save computational power, convergence checks are grouped into batches based on the samplesPerBatch parameter. In each batch, we calculate the mean and variance with the aforementioned tracking variables, and thus our confidence interval. If we have enough confidence (95%) that the pixel has converged, we stop and continue to the next.
